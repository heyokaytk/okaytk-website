# DJ OKAY TK’s Guide to Organizing Your Music Library

**Yo, DJ OKAY TK here.** I’ve been DJing for years, and my music library sits around 600 GB deep. That’s thousands of tracks—so yeah, I’ve had to develop a clean, functional system. If you’re new to DJing, this is how I keep it sharp, simple, and ready for any gig.

## My System: Simple, Structured, and All Caps

I keep my folder structure clean: organized by **genre**, then **decade**. That’s it.

This is what it looks like:

![serato library panel showing the music categories](images/library-panel-decade.png)

No wild subgenres. Simplicity means speed—and that matters in a live set.

Why decades? It’s an easy, logical way to slice up large genres by era​. If someone asks for a 90s hip hop set, I can jump straight into my Hip Hop > 1990s crate and find gold. It also helps me remember the context of tracks. A song from 2003 has a different vibe than one from 2013, even if they’re both hip hop.

## Smart Tagging: Comments, Grouping & 🔥 Tracks

![tracks ](images/tracks.png)

Every track I add gets tagged properly. I use metadata to my advantage:

**Comments**: For sample chains, remix info, or notes like `(8)+(8)+(8)`—which tells me the structure of the intro before the drop.

**Grouping**: Tags like `WEDDING`, `MAIN SET`, or `LOUNGE` help me search by vibe.

Oh, and if you see a **🔥 emoji**? That means it's a straight banger. Not just my opinion—it's been proven on the floor. Play H-Town’s “Knockin' Da Boots” at an R&B night and tell me that’s not certified heat.

And yes, **all my track titles are in ALL CAPS**. Why? Three reasons: it’s easier to read in the dark, it became habit after thousands of files, and, yeah—I’m a little OCD with it.

### Set Cue Points:

I load the song and set my cue points. At minimum, I set a cue at the very start (on the first beat). Then I usually set a cue at the start of the first verse or hook (where the vocals or main part kick in) and another at the beginning of the chorus​. If the song has a bridge or a cool breakdown, I’ll set a cue there too​. The idea is to mark all the important transitions in the song. That way, during a set I can jump to the chorus or an instrumental break instantly. I’ve seen some DJs only bother to set one cue (probably just the intro), and I can guess they only play the song up through the first chorus then mix out​. But if you dig deeper, you might find a dope second verse or a breakdown that could hype the crowd – so mark it! More cue points = more options in your mix.

## Sample Chaining: Connecting the Dots

I’m big on linking music history. If a track samples another, I add that info to the comments. For example:

- **BIGGIE – JUICY** → Comment: `MTUME – JUICY FRUIT`
- **MTUME – JUICY FRUIT** → Comment: `BIGGIE – JUICY`

![track list panel with Hypnotize in the search bar](images/sample-chainging.png)

When I search one, both show up. You can nerd out for hours connecting samples, remixes, and flips this way—and it makes for dope set moments too.

[WhoSampled.com](https://www.whosampled.com/) is my go-to when I’m digging into where a track came from—or who flipped it later. It's a great tool for tracing samples, remixes, and all those hidden gems that make music history so wild.
