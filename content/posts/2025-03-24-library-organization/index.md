# How I Organizing Your Music Library

**Yo, DJ OKAY TK here.** I’ve been DJing for years, and my music library is around 600 GB of tracks – **huge**, I know. Over time, I’ve learned a few things about keeping it organized without losing my mind. If you’re a new DJ looking to get your library on point, here’s how I do it. My style is pretty simple and down-to-earth (just like me), but it works. Let’s dive in.

## Why Keeping It Simple Matters

When it comes to organizing music, **simplicity is king**. My system hasn’t changed much over the years – it’s a **basic structure** that I consider a solid framework. I’ve seen DJs who break their library into a million micro-genres and playlists, and honestly, it gets confusing. Think of your music library like your bedroom dresser: you know which drawer your socks are in because **you** put them there in a way that makes sense to you. The same goes for your tracks. If you over-complicate it, even *you* might get lost in your own library.

I keep genres broad and straightforward. There are so many genres these days, but if you’re just starting out, **stick to the basics** – hip hop, R&B, house, rock/alternative, maybe “retro” for older classics. Don’t create a new crate for “east coast underground conscious hip-hop from 1996 on Tuesdays” – that’s overkill. Start with broad categories, and later on you can break things down further if needed. Keeping it simple means you’ll always know where to find a track when you need it.

## My Folder Structure: Genres & Decades

Let me break down my folder (crate) structure. I organize by **genre first, then by decade**. For example, one of my main folders is **“Club Hip Hop/Rap”**, and inside that I have sub-folders by decade: **1980s**, **1990s**, **2000s**, **2010s**, etc. I do this for each major genre. So, imagine a structure like:

- **Hip Hop / Rap**  
  - 1980s  
  - 1990s  
  - 2000s  
  - 2010s  

- **R&B**  
  - 1980s  
  - 1990s  
  - 2000s  
  - 2010s  

- **House** (and EDM)  
  - 1990s  
  - 2000s  
  - 2010s  

*(You get the idea – broad genre, then break into decades.)* 

Why decades? It’s an easy, logical way to slice up large genres by era. If someone asks for a 90s hip hop set, I can jump straight into my Hip Hop > 1990s crate and find gold. It also helps me remember the context of tracks. A song from 2003 has a different vibe than one from 2013, even if they’re both hip hop.

I generally **avoid subdividing by too many subgenres**. For instance, I don’t separate “Trap” from “Boom Bap” into completely different folders – they all live under Hip Hop, sorted by decade. This keeps things from getting too fragmented. As your library grows, you might add a few sub-crates for specific needs, but start simple. You’ll know where everything is at a glance.

## Tagging with Metadata: Comments & Grouping

One of the **biggest changes** I made to my organization over the years is focusing on metadata – those tags inside your MP3 files. Trust me, a little effort here goes a long way. I **make sure every track has proper tags**, especially **Genre**, and I heavily use the **Comments** and **Grouping** fields. This might sound tedious, but if you do it from the start, you won’t have to fix thousands of songs later.

So what do I put in comments and grouping? **Extra info that helps me DJ.** For example, I’ll tag the **original sample or remix info** in the comment (more on that in a second). I might use the grouping field for quick descriptors like “warm-up set” or “high energy” or for tagging songs that belong to a special series or routine. The idea is: beyond artist/title/genre, use those fields to store anything that helps you find or remember what’s special about the track.

When I scroll through my library, I want to know **at a glance** what a track is about. Often I’ll even include brief notes in the song title or comment. For instance, I sometimes append a code like **“#8”** at the end of a track name to remind me it has an 8-bar intro before vocals drop. That way, I know I’ve got a good instrumental lead-in for a mix without even loading the track. It’s all about giving yourself cues so you’re not scrambling in the middle of a set. *(Shout out to DJ Starting From Scratch – the legend who put me onto that little trick years ago.)*

## Linking Songs by Samples (Sample Chains)

One very cool (and nerdy) thing I do with the comment tag is **“sample chaining.”** Basically, I link songs that sample each other by using the comments. For example, The Notorious B.I.G.’s “Hypnotize” famously samples Herb Alpert’s music. In the comment for **“Hypnotize,”** I’ll note “Herb Alpert – Rise (Original Sample)”. Conversely, in Herb Alpert’s track’s comment, I tag “Notorious B.I.G. – Hypnotize.” Now, whenever I search for one, **both songs show up** in my results because the names are connected through the comments.

I do this for tons of tracks. Take Biggie’s **“Juicy,”** which samples Mtume’s **“Juicy Fruit.”** I put **“Mtume – Juicy Fruit”** in Biggie’s comment, and **“The Notorious B.I.G. – Juicy”** in Mtume’s comment. Now if I type “Juicy” in my library search, I get both the original and the Biggie track – instant link. Over time, I’ve built entire **chains of samples**. It’s like falling down a musical rabbit hole: one song leads to the next, to the next, and so on. It’s actually a fun way to discover and remember music history. 

Does this help at a gig? Maybe, maybe not – I’m not dropping Herb Alpert at a peak hour club set 😅. But it’s great for **knowledge and for creative sets**. If I’m doing a laid-back bar gig or a streaming set on Twitch, I can bust out an original sample followed by the hit that sampled it, and people go “Ohhh snap!” when they recognize it. It adds depth to your repertoire. Plus, as a music lover, it’s just satisfying to **know the roots** of the tracks you’re playing.

## Importing New Tracks: My 3-Minute Routine

Whenever I get new music, I have a little routine to get it prepped and ready to play. It usually takes me about **2-4 minutes per track** from download to fully organized in the library. Here’s what I do for each new song:

1. **Add to the Right Folder/Crate:** First, I drop the file into my main music folder and then import it into my DJ software (Serato, etc.). I immediately place it into the appropriate **genre** crate and **decade/year** sub-crate. If it’s a brand new 2025 release, it goes into my “Hip Hop 2020s” or specifically a **“2025” crate for new stuff**. Organizing by genre & year right away means I’ll know where to find it later.

2. **Tag Metadata (Comments/Grouping):** Next, I edit the track’s info. I make sure the **genre tag** is set correctly and add any notes in the **Comment** and **Grouping** fields. If the song contains a famous sample or I want to remember something about it (like “Clean Intro” or “90s vibe” or a sample chain link), I put that in the comment.

3. **Set Cue Points:** Finally, I load the song and set my **cue points**. At minimum, I set a cue at the very start (on the first beat). Then I usually set a cue at the start of the first verse or hook and another at the beginning of the chorus. If the song has a bridge or a cool breakdown, I’ll set a cue there too.

After these steps, the track is **gig-ready**. I can trust that it’s in the right place in my library and that I won’t forget what it is or where the best parts are.

## Stems vs. Acapellas: My Thoughts on New Tech

Let’s talk tech for a second. With DJ software these days, we have features like **Stems** that can isolate vocals or drums on the fly. It’s wild – you can make an instant acapella or instrumental with the click of a button. I won’t lie, this has made live mashups and transitions **easier** than ever.

However, I’m a bit old-school at heart. I came up hunting down **real acapella** tracks and instrumentals, and I still love using those. The new stem separation tech is great, especially if an official acapella is hard to find. It’s not perfect yet, but it’s getting better all the time. For me though, I often **prefer doing it manually**: I’ll load the actual acapella MP3 on one deck and the instrumental on the other, beatmatch them, and create my remix live.

There’s just something about the sound of an original studio acapella – crisp and full – versus a software-generated one. And beyond sound, it’s about the vibe: doing it myself keeps me **in the moment**. Every time a new “auto-DJ” feature comes out, I feel like a bit of the craft is getting handed over to the computer. I’m not against technology (I use stems when I need to), but I believe in not letting the tech rob you of the **DJ skill**.

## Preparing for Gigs: Freestyle vs. Planning

Early in my career, I used to just **wing it** at gigs. That can work if you know your music well, but let me tell you, a little preparation can elevate your set from good to legendary.

If I know I have a big gig or a specific themed event coming up, I’ll **create a new crate for that event** and start dropping tracks into it in the days or weeks beforehand. These are songs I **might** play – not a fixed set list, but my toolbox for the night.

Why do this if I already have an organized library? Because no matter how good your memory is, when you’re scrolling live during a gig, you will **forget gems**. Prepping a crate helps me remember “oh right, I have this remix that would be perfect.” It doesn’t mean I’m going to play everything in that crate, but it’s there as my curated selection to draw from.

Some DJs brag about **“I never prepare, I just go with the flow.”** That’s cool if it works for them, but I believe for certain gigs you’re doing yourself a disservice if you don’t prepare at least a little. When you prep, you can craft some **special moments**. Those moments are what make a set memorable.

That said, you don’t need to pre-plan every gig to death. For your regular bar night or a casual lounge set, you can rely on your well-sorted library to freestyle no problem. But for big shows or high-stakes sets, I’ll spend the extra time to **step up my game**.

## Trimming the Library: The Favorite T-Shirt Dilemma

Alright, one last topic that every DJ wrestles with: **too much music.** Yes, I said my library is ~600 GB, and it is. But I’m the first to admit that not every one of those songs is a *keeper*. Every so often, I go into purge mode and try to trim the fat. I’m actually doing that now – getting my library “summer ready,” trying to shed those extra files like pounds before beach season.

Cleaning up your library is *hard*. It’s like trying to throw out your favorite old t-shirt that you never wear but can’t let go of. I have tracks that I haven’t played in years… but I **love** them. Maybe it’s a guilty pleasure song or something with sentimental value. Part of me goes, “Man, I can’t delete that, it’s a classic.” It’s just like seeing that ratty t-shirt in your closet – you suddenly imagine wearing it again, even though it’s been in a box in the basement for ages.

What I’ve learned is to be **realistic**. If a song hasn’t made it into any of my crates or sets in the last year, and it’s not a song I truly adore personally, do I really need it taking up space? A leaner library means faster searches, less clutter, and you focusing on the *music that matters*.

---

**Wrapping Up:** That’s the rundown of how I manage my music library. It’s nothing super fancy – in fact, that’s the point. **Keep it simple, tag your tracks well, prepare when it counts, and don’t be afraid to declutter.** Your library is like your toolbox as a DJ. Organize it in a way that when you reach in, you find exactly what you need without digging around. My style might not be the only way, but it’s served me well from small gigs to major clubs. Hopefully, some of these tips help you get your library into shape. Now go forth and rock those decks, DJ! 🎧💪
